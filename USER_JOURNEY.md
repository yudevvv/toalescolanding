# TOALESCO User Journey — Experience Map v1.0

## The Persona

**Name:** Ricardo
**Age:** 42
**Business:** "Mueblería El Bosque" — custom furniture workshop, 12 employees
**Location:** Querétaro, México
**Tech literacy:** Uses WhatsApp. Has an email he rarely checks. His nephew "installed the computer." Excel gives him anxiety.
**Pain:** He writes orders on paper. They get lost. Clients wait 3 weeks for quotes. His nephew built a "system" in Google Sheets that broke. He's angry, tired, and suspicious of anyone who says "digital transformation."

**What Ricardo wants:** To stop losing money because of disorganization.
**What Ricardo needs:** To trust that software can solve his specific problem without creating new ones.
**What Ricardo fears:** Wasting money. Being locked into something he doesn't understand. Looking stupid in front of a tech person.

---

## Phase 0: Before the Visit — The Trigger

### State: FRUSTRATED + HOPEFUL

Ricardo's nephew said "there's this agency a friend recommended." Ricardo searches "software para mi negocio Querétaro" on Google.

**Why this search:** He searched locally, not generically. He wants someone who understands Mexican small business, not silicon valley.

**The search result that matters:** TOALESCO appears not because of SEO tricks but because the agency has done work for Carpintería Hernández (a known competitor).

**Mental model:** "If they helped Hernández, maybe they can help me."

---

## Phase 1: First Contact — The Landing (Scenes 1–2)

### Emotional arc: SKEPTICAL → INTRIGUED

### Scene 1: "Una idea."

**What Ricardo sees:** A dark screen. A single point of light. The words "Una idea." That's it.

**What Ricardo feels:** Confusion. "¿Qué es esto? ¿Se trabó?" He doesn't leave. The simplicity is so unusual that he stays for 2 more seconds.

**Why this exists:** Ricardo expects a carousel, a "WE BUILD WEBSITES" banner, stock photos of people shaking hands. By giving him the opposite, we break his expectation. His brain shifts from "scan and dismiss" to "what is this?"

**The risk:** He might leave. That's acceptable. If he leaves in the first 2 seconds, he wasn't ready to change. The ones who stay are the ones who are desperate enough to be curious.

**The hidden signal:** The single dot is not just aesthetic. It encodes: "Everything starts with one idea. Your idea."

### Scene 2: "Se expande."

**What Ricardo sees:** The dot branches into multiple connected points. A network forms. Lines draw themselves like a blueprint.

**What Ricardo feels:** Recognition. "Esto parece un mapa." He doesn't understand the connections intellectually, but he feels that something is being built. The schematic language is universal — even without labels, he knows dots represent things and lines mean they talk to each other.

**Why this exists:** Ricardo needs to understand that software is not magic. It is a system of connected parts. By showing the network forming, we teach him: your problem is not one thing — it is a set of connected things. We solve connected things.

**Decision point:** If he scrolls past this scene, he has implicitly accepted the premise "my business is a system of connected parts." He is now ready for specificity.

---

## Phase 2: The Relationship — Scenes 3–4

### Emotional arc: UNCERTAIN → BEGINNING TO TRUST

### Scene 3: "Toma forma."

**What Ricardo sees:** A labeled diagram. He reads: Landing Pages, Automatización, Software a Medida, SaaS Products, Integraciones.

**What Ricardo feels:** Relief. "Esto lo entiendo." He may not know what "Automatización" means technically, but he sees that it is a category of solution. The words are in Spanish. They are not "digital touchpoints" or "end-to-end ecosystems." They are real things a business needs.

**Why this exists:** Ricardo has reached the "what can you do for me" moment. He needs to see his problem reflected in your capabilities. He scans for a word that matches his pain.

**The matching moment:** His eyes land on "Software a Medida" (custom software). That is why he came. He sees it listed as one node among others — not featured, not highlighted. This is important: by treating custom software as one part of a system rather than The Main Thing, we signal: "This is normal for us. We do this. It's not a special project."

**What he does not see:** Testimonials. Case studies. Pricing. Logos. He is not ready for these. They would trigger his skepticism: "Of course they show their best reviews."

### Scene 4: "Cobra vida."

**What Ricardo sees:** The nodes now have numbers. 98% uptime. 94% satisfaction. 340 projects. The numbers are small, almost too small to read. They seem like measurements on a blueprint, not marketing claims.

**What Ricardo feels:** Calculated trust. He is an artisan. He measures wood in millimeters. He respects numbers when they look like measurements rather than advertising. The data flowing between nodes looks like material moving through a factory.

**Why this exists:** Ricardo needs proof, but he will reject obvious proof. By presenting metrics as schematic measurements (technical, understated, embedded in the diagram), we bypass his skepticism. He is reading a spec sheet, not a testimonial page.

**The hidden architecture:** The numbers are not claims. They are data flowing through the system. The 98% is not "we guarantee 98% uptime" — it is "the system is showing its current measurement." This framing change is everything. Claims trigger defense. Measurements trigger understanding.

**Decision point:** If he reaches this scene, he has spent ~8 seconds on the site. He has watched ideas become numbers. He has learned the TOALESCO alphabet without reading a single paragraph.

---

## Phase 3: The Decision — Scene 5

### Emotional arc: TRUSTING → READY TO RISK

### Scene 5: "Resultados."

**What Ricardo sees:** Two project cards. Not three, not six. Two. Each shows a name ("Carpintería Hernández," "Taller Mecánico Pérez") and a brief description. A testimonial quote. A privacy link. A contact button.

**What Ricardo feels:** "These are people like me." Hernández is a competitor but also a peer. A carpentry workshop. A mechanic shop. Real businesses. The testimonials are short — not paragraphs of praise, but a single line. "Terminamos perdiendo menos tiempo." (We stopped wasting so much time.) This is exactly what Ricardo wants to say. The quote is not about loving the software. It is about the problem being solved.

**Why this exists:** Ricardo needs a final push. But he cannot be pushed with features ("we use React!"). He cannot be pushed with volume ("10,000 happy customers!"). He can only be pushed by seeing someone like him who took the risk and it worked.

**The two-card limit:** Why not more? Because Ricardo would then need to evaluate which case study is most relevant to him. Evaluation causes paralysis. Two cards is enough to say "yes, this is for businesses like yours" without creating choice anxiety.

**The contact button:** It does not say "Schedule a Call" or "Get Started." It says "Contacto." This is the lowest-commitment word possible. Ricardo is not ready to "get started." He is ready to ask a question.

**The privacy link:** Ricardo does not care about GDPR. But he has been burned before — signed up for something, got spam calls. The privacy link exists not to inform him but to signal: "We won't sell your information." He may not click it, but he sees it.

## Phase 4: The Friction — Before Contact

### Emotional arc: READY → ANXIOUS

**The moment before clicking "Contacto":** Ricardo hesitates. He thinks:

1. "¿Van a querer venderme algo caro?" (Will they try to sell me something expensive?)
2. "¿Voy a entender lo que me digan?" (Will I understand what they say?)
3. "¿Y si no funciona para mi negocio?" (What if it doesn't work for my business?)

**Why the site has not addressed these:** Because no site can. These fears are interpersonal. They will be resolved in conversation, not content. The site's job is to get Ricardo to the conversation without triggering additional fears.

**What would make him close the tab:** If the contact form asks for his phone number and budget. He doesn't know his budget. He doesn't know what this costs. A form that demands a budget is a wall.

**What would make him fill the form:** If the form asks only for his name, his business name, and a message. That's it. No phone? Optional. No budget. No "preferred contact time." The form must be smaller than his fear.

---

## Phase 5: The Contact Form Experience

### Emotional arc: ANXIOUS → RELIEVED

**What Ricardo sees:** A minimal form. Three fields. His name. His business name. A text area: "¿En qué podemos ayudarte?"

**What Ricardo feels:** "Oh, puedo escribir lo que sea." The text area has no placeholder text telling him what to write. The absence of instruction is instruction: "Write whatever you need."

**The submission:** When he clicks "Enviar," the button does not show a spinner. It does not say "Sending..." The button transforms: it compresses (0.97 scale), then the text becomes a checkmark. Duration: 480ms. No loading bar. The system has acknowledged receipt without pretending to work hard.

**What happens next:** A small notification: "Te escribiremos en máximo 24 horas." (We'll write within 24 hours.) This is not a promise of speed. It is a containment of anxiety. Ricardo now knows: if 24 hours pass without contact, something is wrong. He has permission to follow up.

**Why no email confirmation:** Ricardo checks his email twice a week. A confirmation email would create confusion ("¿Por qué me llegó esto?"). The confirmation must happen on the site, in the moment.

---

## Phase 6: The First Response (Hours Later)

### Emotional arc: HOPEFUL → CAUTIOUS

**Ricardo receives a WhatsApp message:** (Not email. WhatsApp is where Ricardo lives.) The message is from a person — a real name, a real photo. Not "TOALESCO Team." Not a salesperson's generic headshot.

**The message says:** "Hola Ricardo, soy Ana de TOALESCO. Recibí tu mensaje sobre el software para tu mueblería. ¿Te parece si hablamos mañana a las 10 am por aquí o prefieres llamada?"

**Why this works:**
- It comes within 24 hours (trust maintained)
- It comes from a real person (trust built)
- It offers a choice of medium (Ricardo controls the interaction)
- It references his specific message (he was heard, not entered into a CRM)

**What Ricardo feels:** "Okay. Estos son serios." The worst outcome (no response, or an automated response) did not happen.

---

## Phase 7: The Conversation

### Emotional arc: CAUTIOUS → PARTNERED

**This document does not design the conversation.** But it defines what the conversation must protect:

1. **Ricardo must never feel stupid.** Every technical term must be immediately followed by a plain-language translation. Not as a "for dummies" explanation, but as a natural alternative: "Un dashboard — una pantalla donde ves todo junto."

2. **Ricardo must control the timeline.** Not "we can build this in 3 weeks" but "¿Para cuándo necesitas tenerlo listo?"

3. **Ricardo must understand the price structure before he asks.** Ana should volunteer: "Proyectos como este cuestan entre $X y $Y. Te doy un presupuesto exacto después de entender bien tu caso." The number anchors his expectation. The range gives him room.

4. **Ricardo must have something physical.** After the call, Ana sends a PDF. Not a proposal — a one-page summary of what they discussed, with a hand-drawn diagram of the proposed system. Ricardo can print it. He can show his nephew. He can hold it.

---

## Phase 8: The Trust Architecture (Post-Engagement)

**After Ricardo becomes a client, the experience continues:**

1. **Progress without meetings.** Ricardo does not want weekly calls. He wants a link where he can see: "Order List — In Progress. Quote Generator — Testing. Sign-off — Pending." This is the system diagram from Scene 3, but now it's his system. His nodes. His connections.

2. **Problems communicated immediately.** If a delay happens, Ricardo gets a WhatsApp: "Oye Ricardo, lo del generador de cotizaciones va a tomar dos días más porque encontramos algo que mejora mucho como funciona. ¿Te parece?" The news is about improvement, not failure.

3. **Education without courses.** Ricardo never takes a "software training." Instead, each feature arrives with a 30-second video of Ana's face explaining it. "Este botón aquí hace que..." Ricardo watches it on his phone.

4. **The final delivery.** When the software is done, Ricardo gets: (a) The working software, (b) A 3-minute video showing everything, (c) A business card with his "contact" — Ana's direct number. Not support@toalesco.com. Ana's number.

## Why Every Part Exists (The Rationale Index)

| Site Element | Exists Because |
|-------------|----------------|
| Dark background with single dot | Breaks "agency template" expectation. Encodes "your idea is the beginning." Filters out non-serious visitors. |
| Blueprint aesthetic | Ricardo trusts plans and measurements. A blueprint says "we engineer" without saying it. |
| Spanish labels | Ricardo does not speak "tech English." Every term must be in his language or he will feel excluded. |
| No pricing on site | Ricardo is not comparing prices. He is deciding whether to trust. Pricing too early triggers comparison, not trust. |
| No testimonials until scene 5 | Testimonials shown too early trigger skepticism. They must be earned by the journey first. |
| Two case studies, not ten | More choices create paralysis. Two is "yes, this applies to me." |
| Contact form with 3 fields | Every additional field is a reason to leave. The form must be smaller than his anxiety. |
| WhatsApp follow-up (not email) | Email is where business goes to die for Ricardo. WhatsApp is where his real life operates. |
| Real person responds (not CRM) | Ricardo has been ignored by automated systems. A real person's name is the most valuable asset at this stage. |
| One-page PDF summary after call | Ricardo needs something physical. He cannot process information that only exists in memory. PDF is the new "handshake." |
| Progress dashboard (post-sale) | Ricardo does not want to call for updates. A visual dashboard lets him check without asking. He controls access to information. |
| 30-second feature videos | Ricardo will not read documentation. He will watch a short video from a face he trusts. |
| Direct number for support | Ricardo will not open a ticket. He will message the person he knows. This is not inefficient — it is the relationship working as designed. |

## The Non-Negotiables

1. **No English unless user switches to it.** Never assume bilingualism.
2. **No pricing without context.** Numbers without scope create fear.
3. **No auto-play video.** Ricardo is on a limited data plan on his phone.
4. **No chatbot.** Ricardo has been frustrated by chatbots. If he wants to talk, he wants a person.
5. **No "book a demo."** Ricardo does not know what a demo is. "Hablemos" (let's talk) is the only CTA.
6. **No login wall.** Before contacting, Ricardo must see everything. Nothing hidden behind "sign up to see pricing."
7. **No jargon in case studies.** "Migrated to cloud-native architecture" must become "Pasamos todo a internet para que funcione desde cualquier lugar."

---

## How the Site Supports the Journey (Without Being a Funnel)

The site is not a funnel. Funnels push people toward a destination. The site is a **permission structure** — a series of moments where the visitor gives themselves permission to continue.

- **Permission 1:** "This looks different. Let me see what it is." (Scene 1)
- **Permission 2:** "Okay, I understand the metaphor. Show me what you do." (Scene 2)
- **Permission 3:** "I see my problem in your categories. Tell me more." (Scene 3)
- **Permission 4:** "The numbers look real. Show me proof." (Scene 4)
- **Permission 5:** "These are people like me. Maybe I should ask." (Scene 5)
- **Permission 6:** "The form is simple. I can write what I need." (Contact)
- **Permission 7:** "A real person responded. I can trust this." (Response)

Each permission is granted by the user, not extracted by the system. The system's job is to create the conditions for permission — by being clear, honest, and respectful of Ricardo's intelligence and his fears.

**End of User Journey v1.0**

*Every pixel, every word, every interaction exists to move Ricardo from "I have a problem" to "I trust you to solve it." If a feature does not serve this journey, it does not belong on the site.*
